# study-of-panther-poop
A field-oriented, educational study of panther scat (droppings, feces, and other signs). Professional, science-forward, and yes — a little bit playful. This repository collects observations, morphology notes, habitat/diet clues, and ethical field practices for naturalists, students, and researchers.

---

## Overview
This project documents characteristics of panther scat to support wildlife biology, non-invasive monitoring, and environmental education. The goal is to provide clear, replicable notes that help distinguish panther sign from other carnivores, infer diet and territory use, and inform safe, ethical field work.

Who this is for:
- Field biologists and students learning scat-based monitoring techniques
- Conservationists conducting non-invasive surveys
- Naturalists and educators creating outreach material
- Responsible citizen scientists working with partners and permits

What this is not:
- A how-to guide for culturing or handling biological hazards
- Instruction for doing anything that requires a permit (collection, genetic sampling, etc.) without appropriate legal/ethical oversight

---

## Why it matters
Panther scat is a compact source of ecological information:
- Diet: fragments of bone, hair, and plant matter reveal prey choices and seasonality.
- Territory & behavior: placement and frequency of scat can indicate marking behavior and movement corridors.
- Non-invasive monitoring: scat provides genetic and dietary data without capturing animals.
- Conservation value: in elusive species (e.g., large felids), scat-based data are often the most practical source of information.

Accurate scat identification supports better management decisions and stronger conservation outcomes.

## Morphology notes
Key morphological features to record when observing scat in the field:

- Shape & form:
  - Typically cylindrical or sausage-like; may be tapered or blunt-ended.
  - Can be broken into segments or appear as a single continuous mass.
- Size:
  - Record length and diameter (use a ruler or photo scale). Panthers often produce larger, thicker scats than small felids.
- Color & age:
  - Fresh: dark, glossy, and moist.
  - Aged: faded, flaky, bleached by sun exposure; texture changes with weather.
- Contents:
  - Hair: often abundant when prey is fur-bearing; hair may form a plug or be mixed throughout.
  - Bone fragments: crunchy and radiopaque in photos; indicate larger prey or consumption of carcasses.
  - Plant material: seeds, berries, or fruit remnants suggest seasonal omnivory/foraging on fruit.
- Odor & consistency:
  - Fresh scat from carnivores is typically strong-smelling; note but do not inhale closely.
  - Consistency ranges from firm to semi-formed depending on diet and hydration.
- Comparison cues:
  - Distinguish from canid scat: canids often have segmented, twisted ends and may contain more plant matter; dogs may have more uniform tubular shapes.
  - Distinguish from smaller felids (bobcat): smaller diameter, less bone content, less volume.

Record metadata for each observation:
- GPS coordinates, date/time, habitat description, observer, distance from trail, and any nearby sign (tracks, scrapes, hair).

## Habitat / diet clues
Scat is a snapshot of recent ecology. Useful interpretations include:

- Prey size inference:
  - Lots of bone and large hair clumps — consumption of medium-to-large mammals.
  - Small mammal hair & small bones — rodents, rabbits, or small ungulates.
- Seasonal diet shifts:
  - Fruit seeds/plant fibers indicate use of fruiting plants (seasonal).
  - More fur and bone in colder months may reflect reliance on mammals.
- Habitat signals:
  - Scat along travel routes, game trails, or creek crossings often indicates habitual use.
  - Scat piles at prominent sites can indicate territorial marking.
- Human/wildlife interactions:
  - Presence of anthropogenic items (plastic, processed food) signals human-wildlife overlap and possible conflict.

Interpretation best when combined with other signs (tracks, camera-trap images, prey remains) and with knowledge of local species assemblages.

## Ethics / Safety
Safety and ethics are paramount. Follow these guidelines:

- Legal & permitting:
  - Obtain necessary permits before collecting samples for analysis (DNA, stable isotopes, etc.).
  - Respect protected species regulations and private property.
- Non-invasive first:
  - Prefer photography, measurement, and environmental notes over removal.
  - If collection is necessary, coordinate with accredited labs and wildlife authorities.
- Biosafety precautions:
  - Treat scat as potentially infectious. Do not open or culture samples in the field.
  - Use basic PPE: nitrile gloves, disposable bags, and eye protection as appropriate.
  - Avoid inhaling dust or aerosols from dried scat; use a mask if disturbance is likely.
  - Decontaminate tools and hands after handling; dispose of gloves/bags properly.
- Animal welfare:
  - Do not disturb animals, dens, or nests to obtain scat.
  - Avoid baiting or manipulating the environment to attract animals.
- Data ethics:
  - Anonymize exact locations of endangered species in public reports to prevent poaching or disturbance.
  - Share sensitive data responsibly with conservation partners.

If in doubt, consult local wildlife agencies, institutional biosafety officers, or experienced field biologists.

## Future work
Planned directions and collaboration opportunities:

- Build a standardized field datasheet and mobile-friendly form for consistent recording.
- Curate an anonymized, peer-reviewed repository of labeled scat images to aid training of students and automated classifiers.
- Partner with labs for non-invasive genetic sampling protocols and best-practice guidance.
- Develop outreach materials (field guides, posters, short workshops) for community science programs.
- Integrate scat data with camera-trap, GPS-collar, and habitat data to produce richer ecological insights.
- Explore safe, permitted uses of environmental DNA (eDNA) from scat for diet and pathogen surveillance.

Contributions welcome! Please open issues or pull requests to propose improvements, datasets, or teaching modules. For sensitive data or research collaboration, contact the repository maintainers before submitting.
